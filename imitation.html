Imitating Professional and Academic Genres

Hannah Arendt from The Human Condition


In the following section, I examine how SPN affiliates adopt academic and professional genres to produce and circulate disinformation in the form of fake news, spin, falsehoods, and misleading information and explain why this tactic is so effective. Genre theory is a valuable tool for investigating how disinformation networks rely on conventions to persuade audiences and it is the most valuable theory from writing studies for interrogating disinformation culture. SPN affiliates work together “in coordinated efforts to push their agenda, often using the same cookie-cutter research and reports” (The Center for Media and Democracy, p. 1) that imitate professional genres to enhance their credibility. Stasiuk-Krajewska (2024) studied fake news as a genre more closely and argues that it is a primary form for disinformation networks (p. 59). Fake news is a type of disinformation that can be used to achieve long terms shifts in how people think or trust information. She contends that certain genres from journalism and academia are particularly viable for housing and spreading disinformation. Genres like a "mention, note, announcement, report, correspondence, biography, press review, news, . . . infographics" are all common forms that bad actors implement to mislead people (Stasiuk-Krajewska, p. 61). Pierre (2025) reiterates how bad actors mimic journal titles, conventions, and styles to hide disinformation (p. 55). Academic and professional are highly epistemic and represent how knowledge is made in a field. The SPN relies on these types of genres to endorse misleading claims that validate its agenda. Publishing misleading content online does not require an intricate and complex network of peer-reviewed feedback, fact-checking, and verification processes. Shanthi Kalathil (2020) emphasizes that "disinformation outlets" will "disguise themselves as independent journalism while failing to adhere to standard, best-practice accountability measures, such as bylines, mastheads, verification, corrections, and community service principles" (p. 35). Publishing disinformation on websites allows the SPN to avoid formal gatekeeping and quality control from experts and standard verification procedures that are expected from reputable and nationally renowned publication outlets. The longevity of disinformation is dependent on how well it resembles healthy information. The consistent production of misleading content that has been debunked is a characteristic of a disinformation network. In "Disinformation in the Regan Years and Lessons for Today," Reiss (2019) explains how the Soviets used homemade "scientific research" to create and circulate conspiracies in a different genres and publication outlets across the media (p. 4).

The SPN uses peer-reviewed journal articles, research reports, white papers, editorials, news, policy reports, reviews, and graphs and charts to embed misleading information into an alternative media culture. Every SPN website contains a research or publication page that provides audiences with access to numerous types of professional documents that push the same target issues across all platforms, creating a network for triangulating and coordinating disinformation. The publication link on the navigation bar on each website provides access to its authoritative genres. For example, in figure 1, the Competitive Enterprise Institute's (CEI) website has additional links on its navigation bar for users to access studies, testimonies, and coalition letters. The presence of these genres symbolize the presence of credibility, but the CEI is known for producing disinformation. Bennett and Livingston (2021b) highlight that the CEI's biggest donor is Exxon, and that it publshes disinformation on its website to "undermine science and the weight of factbased discourse" (p. 273).

The SPN relies on the well-established respect for professional and academic genres to disguise disinformation. Building disinformation for the SPN starts with using authoritative genres. Structure relates to how messages are "apportioned and sequenced" (Hart & Daughton, p. 103), and form is related to "patterns of meaning audiences generate" from the message structure (Hart & Daughton, p. 104). Genres are the product of specific organizational methods for structuring content that lead audiences to form meaning from those structures. Audiences locate meaning within the structures of the text, so the structures can become a representation of knowledge and credibility without a comprehensive understanding of the content. Bawarshi (2003) emphasizes that "Structures, in short, are both the ideology and the enactment of the ideology" (p. 88) regardless of the content. Bawarshi and Reiff (2010) reiterate that "Genre provides the ideological context in which a text and its users function, relate to other genres and texts, and attain cultural value" (p. 27). Genres evoke an ideological context that shapes how readers interpet content.

It is the structures of a professional and academic genre that signal to audiences a certain quality of information exists. Structures evoke an ideological epistemology and expectation. Obfuscating misleading content within the structures of a genre is an extremely effective disinformation tactic because genres dictate how audiences generate meaning. As "a class of messages sharing important structural and content features," genres cater to the "special expectations in an audience" and provide them with patterns for meaning making that are well-known (Hart & Daughton, p. 104). Privileged and authoritative genres that promote misleading information can satisfy an audience's expectations for trust and prevent it from recognizing disinformation. Genres are a primary medium for hiding and circulating problematic content because genres form "special expectations in an audience" (Hart & Daughton, p. 104) and become systematized, operationalized, and invisible over time. Imitating an academic or professional genre manufactures the illusion of credibility. Understanding how disinformation mimics authoritative genres is a form of crap detection.

Professional and academic genres are “codified materiality" that gets "anchored in texts, behaviors, and composing habits” that many people rely on to determine trustworthiness (Anderson and Sayers, 2015 p. 83). Seeing these genres embedded on a website signals that a specific codified materiality of actions and behaviors took place to bring them into existence. A closer examination of SPN websites exposes how it relies on genres to transport misleading content. Affiliates like the Mackinac Institute have a publications page that identifies these genres, and the Illinois Policy Forum (IPF) website has a specific page for research reports that contain homegrown research. The Journal of Markets and Morality is another example of how the SPN imitates an academic peer-reviewed journal to acquire ethos. On the surface, the journal appears to be a credible publication outlet, with a focus on using religion and morals to evaluate, critique, and design economic policies. However, the journal is funded by the Acton Institute, an SPN affiliate, and many of the articles promote the same SPN narrative and questionable claims found on SPN websites. What makes this an effective disinformation tactic is that the content of the articles came from scholars and is peer-reviewed. Kress and van Leeuwen (2006) elaborate on how the imitation process works, noting that "neutral, purely informative (linguistic) texts which emerge in newspaper reporting, government publications, social science reports, and so on, realize, articulate and disseminate 'discourses' as ideological positions just as much as do texts which more explicitly editorialize or propagandize” (p. 14). Relying on specific types of professional genres to distribute and circulate disinformation is a form of ideological positioning that takes advantage of and, preys on, an audience's respect for professional genres. The conventions for authoritative genres become the representations of the form, and the presence of these conventions becomes a representation for honesty. An effective method for learning how to identify disinformation is to use genre theory as a frame to analyze it.

The content and focus of the publications in this journal are unlike the publications found in accredited peer-reviewed journals, but some of the structures of an academic journal are distinctly present. Again, using the term journal evokes the "powerful epistemic and ontological functions" of academic research publications and rigor (McNely and Teston, 2015, p. 114). Moreover, content can acquire the illusion of ethos if it is structured with a specific set of conventions in a particular way. Bawarshi explains that "Structures both provide a defined, socially recognized, and ideological action-potential (what individuals can do in a given situation) as well as the means of instantiating that potential as actualized social practice in space and time" (p. 89). It is the ideological action-potential of a genre that can mask disinformation because it instantiates the borders and boundaries that control the genre.

This disclaimer is a genre of stability that tries to establish the IPF as a place to go for verified research and scholarship. The disclaimer states that the IPF follows the rules for producing and sharing honest information. Miller (1994) explains that “The rules and resources of a genre provide reproducible speaker and addressee roles” that feel stable and reliable (p. 71); they are "preservative; they keep established social patterns viable" (Hart & Daughton, p. 119), and they provide a "cultural rationality” (Miller, p. 165) for adopting those social patterns. Moreover, "Genres are conservative. They keep things in place. To speak in established ways by following the rules is to tip one's hat to the forces-that-be" even if what is said in those established ways is false (Hart & Daughton, p. 117). If it appears someone followed the rules for a specific discourse, there may be less questions about its content. For these reasons, the SPN creates homegrown research to support misleading claims in these genres that both conserve and preserve patterns of reactions and responses.
