Hannah Arendt from The Human Condition


Disinformation has become more complex and well-funded. Wealthy individuals with political motives are financing the spreading of misleading content across numerous media outlets. Privately funded and politically active think tanks and organizations use nonprofit 503(c) status to form media companies in each state and design "misinformation ecosystems" (Hardy, p. 61). Disinformation that informs political decisions is especially problematic. One current concern with disinformation research is that it has not drawn enough attention to the financing of disinformation networks that seek to reshape political and cultural narratives with misleading content and influence state and national legislation. Echeverría et al. and Bennett and Livingston (2021b) recommend, along with this webtext, more research on how politically motivated groups and wealthy donors are financing disinformation networks that influence state legislation. Establishing more critical definitions of disinformation is also vital for identifying and studying how it functions in different contexts, platforms, and media outlets. In this webtext, I argued that Cheyfitz's definition for disinformation more accurately identifies how it is a blueprint for the long term disruption of the public's ability to think and the replacement of well-established facts with alternative realities. The SPN implements click-bait headlines, misleading images and graphs, fraudulent primary research, fake sourcing, and populist rhetoric to disinform and achieve long term, political goals. These tactics generate layers of disinformation that are extraordinarily difficult to unravel because they can strengthen its authority and power. Anderson and Sayers (2015) posit that "Layers afford precisely that opportunity for unpacking the ambiguous (dis)continuities, versions, and relational pressure points" (p. 82) that make disinformation difficult to see, hear, or recognize when first encountering it. This is why it is important to expand how disinformation is defined.

This webtext also asserts that critical literacies and genre theory are particularly helpful for locating and analyzing disinformation networks. Interpreting how disinformation is networked is a form of high-order crap detection that requires technical literacies and a thorough understanding of genre theory. To extend the definition of disinformation, I analyzed how the SPN manufactures a national disinformation network that influences state legislation. I outlined how the SPN aligns with Cheyfitz definition for disinformation and examined a range of tactics the SPN employs to achieve its long term goals. The research on disinformation and misinformation has not paid close enough attention to disinformation networks or provided specific examples of networked disinformation across genres, modes, and mediums. To demonstrate how writing studies can contribute to scholarship on disinformation, I used genre theory and critical literacy theory as frames to interpet and analyze disinformation across SPN websites. Genre theory is particularly useful for analyzing disinformation because it exposes how structures and forms for genre instantiate values and authority that alters how the content is represented. To avoid becoming entangled in a network of disinformation, educational institutions need to redefine and recultivate a new type of intellectual craftsperson (Mills, 1959) for the current disinformation culture who can use writing studies to investigate and interrogate information. The intellectual craftsperson is a term Charles Mills used to describe the habits of mind reflective thinkers adopt to examine, interpret, engage, and analyze their world. An intellectual craftsperson in 2026 has to have "epistemic vigilance" (Pierre, p. 65). Pierre notes that Epistemic vigilance is a term to identify how people learn to trust something. An intellectual craftsperson who has epistemic vigilance is skeptical of their own thinking and relies on critical literacies and writing studies to learn, think, and design their way through a complex media ecology. Disinformation has created an important opportunity for scholars who study writing, literacies, and rhetoric to promote the acquisition of technical literacies and outline ethical behaviors and epistemologies for writing and thinking. Determining what is factual and honest requires a craftsperson with a critical mindest.
