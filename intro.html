Introduction

This webtext examines how the State Policy Network (SPN) manufactures a network of disinformation across the US in all 50 states. The SPN is a conservative think tank primarily funded by the Koch brothers. According to Sourcewatch.org, there are over 160 members in the SPN registered as a 503(c)(3) nonprofit. The SPN consults with industry and the American Legion Executive Committee (ALEC) to write model bills for state legislation. For this webtext, I analyzed how 64 SPN affiliates use websites to function as a disinformation network. Zimdars and McLeod (2020) assert that disinformation is most effective when it is part of "a vast network of propagandistic and fake news websites" (p. 3). Disinformation networks perpetuate misleading narratives for political influence over sustained periods of time. Cheyfitz (2019) explains that “Disinformation is a deep, historical process of erasing history itself, culminating in a disruption or blockage of critical thinking in which particular fictions, through repeated and widespread use in our major institutions (schools, media, government, and political parties), substitute reflexively for facts” (p. 49). This is the definition of disinformation I adopt to frame this webtext's analysis of a disinformation network.

The SPN functions as a disinformation network that uses a range of tactics to replace truths with alterantive facts. Disinformation is much more than intentional deciet and lies; it is a slow, deliberate, and consistent undermining of selective truths, using a variety techniques like misinformation, fake news, spin, propaganda, and falsehoods that promote the financial and political intentions of certain corporations, industries, and politicians. Disinformation takes intentionally misleading people a step further and seeks to substitute the truth with falsehoods over time, transforming reality. The process for substituting truth with lies is a primary characteristic of disinformation. Another characteristic of disinformation is repetition. Tumber and Waisbord contend that disinformation is dependent on a “transnational matrix” (p. 5) of bad actors who rely on misleading information, spin, and fake news and evidence to achieve their long term goal. The SPN perpetuates a series of misleading narratives that benefit industry, corporations, and political organizations. In "Why and How the Koch Network Uses Disinformation to Thwart Democracy," Nancy MacLean (2021) reiterates that the most significant producer of disinformation in the US is "the network of extreme right donors, allied organizations, and academic grantees convened over decades by Charles Koch" (p. 121). MacLean contends that even though the SPN will engage audiences in dialogue, all Koch-backed networks "rely on disinformation" to push its narratives and financial interests "where persuasion has failed" (p. 123).

The SPN deliberately produces and circulates misleading information to undermine and displace facts with alternative truths. For example, the SPN perpetuates inaccurate and false information about election integrity on all of its websites. "Koch-funded organizations" continue to "spread the falsehood of mass voter fraud . . . even after repeated studies have exposed it to be a non-existent problem" (MacLean, p. 135). It is this continued and deliberate effort to perpetuate false narratives that makes the SPN a disinformation network. The SPN's ties to state legislation and wealthy donors make it especially important to critique. Examining how disinformation shapes legislation should be a priority. MacLean reveals that Koch has spent millions of dollars to "mislead the public" with "a quiet, slow-motion revolution" that captures how modern forms of disinformation function (p. 138). Donors are responsible for financing disinformation narratives across legacy and alternative media outlets and using alternative facts to influence state legislation. Slowing down disinformation requires disrupting the financers of disinformation networks. Bakir and McStay (2022) contend that there is plenty of research that demonstrates how "disinformation networks of think tanks, lobbyists and financiers" mislead people (p. 73), but more attention and scholarly evidence is needed on how this process works. Bennett and Livingston (2021b) are concerned with an overfocus on studying the effects of disinformation instead of its causes and financing. They highlight how wealthy elites have paid for the "capture and erosion of governing institutions" because they are "unable to sell their actual agendas to the public without increasing levels of disinformation" (p. 8). Echeverría et al. (2025) assert that "Increased focus needs to be paid to disinformation campaigns that are systematically orchestrated by a collusion of domestic elite actors with the goal of damaging both the robustness of democratic institutions and citizens’ trust in them, while advancing their own goals" (p. 1). The systemic financing, designing, and spreading of disinformation starts and ends with wealthy donors. There is very little academic scholarship on how the SPN relies on wealthy donors to function as a disinformation network and how writing and rhetoric as a field can help critique disinformation and this webtext attempts to fill that gap.

I argue that disinformation is not meant to achieve an immediate response or effect. To extend the definition of disinformation, this webtext analyzes how the SPN manufactures a national disinformation network that influences state legislation. Disinformation seeks epistemic shifts that erode stable truths over time. Getting people to stop using vaccines or to deny climate science takes time. Sowing distrust is the end goal for disinformers. Falsehoods have to be repeated across a wide range of platforms and publication outlets to take root. Intentionally misleading people for an extended length of time to alter truths for political purposes aligns with more current definitions of disinformation. Disinformation is the sustained and continued effort to provide misleading information in many forms, across platforms and publication outlets. There has been significant research on analyzing disinformation and misinformation (Charney, 2001; Karpf, 2019; Jenkins, 2008; Jowett & O'Donell, 2019; Lazer, et al., 2018; Martin, 2020; Marwick & Lewis, 2017), but there has been less attention paid to analyzing disinformation networks, extending the definition of disinformation, and using writing studies to investigate disinformation across genres, modes, and mediums. I used McNely and Teston's (2015) approach to "field work" as a methodology to mark and code the characteristics of a disinformation network. After collecting hundreds of pages of notes and hundreds of screenshots of web content for analysis (McNely & Teston's, 2015, p. 117), I coded and analyzed the most commonly used disinformation tactics I found on the SPN websites. I outline how to recognize and interrogate networked disinformation using several critical literacies. To demonstrate how writing studies can contribute to scholarship on disinformation, I use genre theory (Bawarshi, 2003; Bawarshi & Reiff, 2011; Hart & Doughton, 2005) and critical literacy theory (Craig, 2017; Miller & Leon, 2017; Selber, 2004; Selfe, 1998; Ong, 2002; Sindoni & Moschini, 2022) as frames to interpet and analyze disinformation. I contend that genre theory is particularly useful for analyzing disinformation. Pennycook et al. (2015) contend that people are more likely to believe falsehoods when they are not metacognitive and have poor literacies and language skills. They suggest that analytical writing and verbal intelligence are tools for combatting disinformation. Writing studies is in a position to teach students critical ways of knowing and seeing text and language.

Learning how to identify a disinformation network and how it designs and circulates misleading content across multiple platforms is an important digital literacy needed in the age of disinformation disorders. To learn more about the research for this project, examine the summary of the SPN and use an interactive map to explore SPN websites across the nation. These tactics embody and extend Cheyfitz's definition of disinformation as "a deep, historical process of erasing history itself, culminating in a disruption or blockage of critical thinking in which particular fictions . . . substitute reflexively for facts” (p. 49). Understanding a Disinformation Network

Defining Disinformation, Misinformation, and Fake News

Defining terms like fake news, disinformation, and misinformation is difficult because these terms are nuanced and connect, overlap, and disconnect from each other depending on the context.

Unverified Research

research is funded, created, and published by the SPN but disguised as objective, peer-reviewed research. The SPN conducts its own primary research (surveys, interviews, graphs, charts) to compete with more credible, well-established facts in scientific and professional research communities. Homegrown research is pseudo research that serves the interests of the SPN and helps it manifest an appearance of a credible publication network.

Imitating Genres of Authority

professional and academic genres allows the SPN to disguise disinformation and confuse audiences. The SPN mimics the structures and forms of professional and academic genres and discourse to produce disinformation across its websites.

Circulating Acontexual Data

form spaces for audiences to disinform themselves. Large sets of acontextual data can leave audiences baffled and confused. The SPN publishes and shares acontextual data without a frame to interpret the data.

Establishing Expertise

help disinformation organizations manufacture a professional ethos that doesn't exist. The SPN hires experts with advanced and terminal degrees to enhance its credibility. Experts disguise opinions and anecdotes inside the structures and forms of professional and academic genres.

Redundant Narratives

on all the SPN websites instantiate echo chambers. The continued focus on topics like anti-immigration, global warming skepticism, and privatizing education across the SPN's websites triangulates and enables disinformation.

503(c)

The SPN shields its disinformation behind a nonprofit 503(c). A nonprofit status alludes to the SPN as a beacon of trust and a symbol of honesty.

Impairing Users

drastically slows down their ability to validate source material quickly. Every disinformation tactic on a SPN website is designed to impair the audience and make it more cumbersome to verify content. Obfuscating where sources come from and how they're made makes it time consuming for audiences to vet information.
