Defining Key Terms Associated with Disinformation

">
Hannah Arendt from The Origins of Totalitarianism

An expanding disinformation culture needs more fluid and complex definitions for disinformation, misinformation, and fake news that account for the digital evolution of post-truth culture and the ways in which bad actors network falsehoods across a range of publication outlets and platforms. It is important to acknowledge the growth in application of disinformation across media outlets and contexts and understand how terms like disinformation, misinformation, fake news, and propaganda connect to, overlap with, and separate from each other. The wider body of research on disinformation and misinformation claims that defining these terms is complicated because they are diffuse and unstable. Rebolledo (2025) states that "the high complexity and the multiple dimensions of disinformation as a concept call for a deeper reflection on its applicability to other contexts" (p. 41). Disinformation as a concept has expanded beyond the traditional definitions that have been used to contain and study it. Stasiuk-Krajewska and Wenzel (2024) insist that disinformation is far too "dynamic" and "fluid" to define in one way because of "its application in many practical and research contexts" that serve vastly different rhetorical situations (p. 12). The types of disinformation being invented across a spectrum of platforms and media outlets requires new theories to understand. Echeverría et al. reiterate that defining disinformation as intentionally misleading statements fails to capture how it is a blueprint for a "systematic and coordinated effort . . . to gain political and economic dominance by controlling public discourse and opinions" (p. 3). Disinformation is a term that outlines how bad actors plan to directly and indirectly gain control over media companies and publication outlets and use them to filter disinformation into the public. Bąkowicz (2024) insists that what makes defining disinformation most difficult "is the multitude of tools through which it manifests itself in social communication" (p. 166). Different mediums, modes, and genres to generate and circulate disinformation form specific effects that are complex. Disinformation functions as an umbrella term for deliberately misleading people. Misinformation, fake news, spin, misleading data, and unverified evidence are forms of disinformation that bad actors use to mislead people.

Terms

Disinformation

Stasiuk-Krajewska and Wenzel assert that disinformation is nearly impossible to define because it "is not a precise concept" and "is used for different purposes and with different intentions" across political, cultural, and social contexts (p. 10). Most scholars who study disinformation define it as the sharing of false or misleading information with intent (Armitage and Vaccari, 2021; Bakir & McStay, 2022; Benkler et al., 2018; Iosifidis, 2025; Stasiuk-Krajewska, 2024). Epstein (2021) defines disinformation as "false information that is deliberately created or disseminated with the express purpose to cause harm or make profit" (p. 192). The application and implementation of disinformation across a wide range of contexts complicates how it is defined and expands what it is and how it does what it does. Levy et al. assert that disinformation is the “exploitation of uncertainty" (p., 235), and that a hyper focus on publicizing and exploiting uncertainty over time leads to sowing distrust and replacing facts with falsehoods. Disinformation is the process of normalizing a healthy competition between facts and alternative facts. Tumber and Waisbond (2021) contend that disinformation is “deliberate attempts to sow confusion and misinformation among the public, with the purpose of political gain” (p. 1). Their defintion for disinformation centers intent and political motivation.


Almost all current definitions for disinformation involve some reference to political motives. Bennet and Livingston (2021) contend that disinformation is intentionally spreading misleading information "to advance political goals such as discrediting opponents, disrupting policy debates, influencing voters, inflaming existing social conflicts, or creating a general backdrop of confusion and informational paralysis" (p. 3). This is a much more complex definition that embodies several exigencies. Bakir and McStay also reiterate that the primary incentive for spreading disinformation is "political gain" (p. 175). Zelizer (2024) and Iosifidis (2025) also highlight how disinformation is closely associated with undermining "political stability, financial safety, and mental and physical health" (Zelizer, p. 34). These definitions situate disinformation as a plan to intentionally mislead, deceive, and harm people over time, displace reality, and gain political and economic power. Bad actors use fake news, propoganda, lies, spin, and misinformation to foment disinformation campaigns for political gain (Bąkowicz, 2024). Terms like fake news are a type of disinformation.

Misinformation

Most scholars who study misinformation define it as the sharing of false or misleading information without intent (Armitage and Vaccari, 2021; Bakir & McStay, 2022; Benkler et al., 2018; Iosifidis, 2025; Stasiuk-Krajewska, 2024). For example, Dave et al. (2020) define misinformation as "unintentional inaccurate content" and disinformation as "intentionally false content" (p. 6). Epstein (2021) defines misinformation as "Information that is false, but spread unintentionally and without intent to cause harm" (p. 192). It is intent that separates disinformation and misinformation from each other in these definitions, even though discerning intent can be difficult or impossible. Someone can unintentionally spread false information, but bad actors may have orchestrated that unintentional


spreading of misleading information, so the intent of creating misleading content was to disinform people and get them to share problematic content with their networks. Using intent to determine differences between disinformation and misinformation is difficult because there is no adequate and precise method to measure intent. Audiences are unable to discern intent without some level of transparency, so misinformation and disinformation can easily collapse into each other when defined in these ways, which happens frequently and causes confusion and misuse and misapplication of these terms. Without an adequate method for determining intent, it is impossible to consistently distinguish between these terms. One way to address this concern is to expand the definition of these terms.

Fake News

Stasiuk-Krajewska and Wenzel stress that fake news is a tool for disinforming that imiates journalism. "Fake news is untrue news, disseminated with the intent to deceive or without concern for the truth, but additionally, fake news mimics journalistic information/news" (Stasiuk-Krajewska and Wenzel, p. 12). Epstein (2021) prefers to avoid the term fake news because "It is a confusing and overly broad term that should be minimized in academic work" and research (p. 193). Frissen et al. (2021) contend that fake news as a term is nebulous; it’s a phrase people use to assert that any source or claim they don't agree with or understand lacks credibility.


No news is fake. All news is real, whether or not it reports falsehoods or truths. Frissen et al. believe that it’s more helpful to study what people do with the news and information they obtain than how it’s made. This means educators need to teach students how to think about what they are doing with information and how it affects them and those around them. Zimdars and McLeod (2020) state that fake news is the "constant flow of purposefully crafted sensational, emotionally charged, misleading, or otherwise totally made-up information" (p. 2). It is a "type of online misinformation" (Zimdars and McLeod, p. 3), but misinformation is the spreading of misleading content without intent, according to most definitions, which makes this term unstable. Expanding how terms related to disinformation are defined is important for studying how disinformation functions and for understanding the differences between terms.

Propoganda

Like disinformation, propaganda has numerous definitions and is extremely difficult to define outside of a specific context. Known for reducing complex messages into simple claims, propaganda is a term that scholars can use to learn more about modern disinformation campaigns. Benkler et al. (2018) define propoganda as "Communication designed to manipulate a target population by affecting its beliefs, attitudes, or preferences in order to obtain behavior compliant with political goals of the propagandist" (p. 29). This is similar to definitions for disinformation. Propaganda's association with autocracies instead of democracies has also limited its application and connection to disinformation. However, more complex and modern definitons of disinformation are similar to propaganda. Propaganda relies on some of the same tools as disinformation: falsehoods, spin, misinformation, and distortions. The primary difference between propaganda and disinformation is that not all propaganda is malevolent and designed to intentionally hurt and deceive people. One connection between disinformation and propaganda is that they both reshape realities over long periods of time.


Other connections between propaganda and disinformation and misinformation that are helpful for understanding how disinformation operates. Benkler et al. contend that propaganda and disinformation are similar when they employ intentional deceit "to achieve political ends" (p. 24). They note that network propaganda occurs when a "wide range of outlets" are able to "repeat various versions" of disinformation increasing its "credibility" and "disseminating that narrative more widely in that population" (Benkler et al., p. 34). This model for network propaganda is similar to the SPN disinformation network. Although propoganda and disinformation are similar, propaganda is used in non harmful ways. Misinformation, fake news, spin, falsehoods, and misleading content are tools for both propaganda and disinformation, but disinformation prioritizes undermining inconvenient truths for political and financial gain and intentionally harming people over long periods of time.

Confirmation Bias, Selective Exposure, and Motivated Reasoning

There is an increasing amount of research on disinformation that examines psychological dispositions and susceptiblity to misleading content. The three most common terms from cognitive science associated with disinformation are selective exposure, confirmation bias, and motivated reasoning (Bakir & Mcstay, 2022; Pierre, 2025). Selective exposure is seeking out information that reiterates someone's worldview and value system. Motivated reasoning is the acceptance of misleading information that promotes and privileges someone's worldviews. This theory explains how humans deliberately consume information that supports their perspectives and ideologies, bending the facts to safeguard their views. Confirmation bias is also the seeking out of information that values a specific worldview. Pierre (2025), a psychiatrist who studies disinformation, identifies other relevant terms that are connected to disinformation like “Naive realism” or the notion that whatever someone sees in front of them is the truth (p. 9). Knowledgeable people tend to underestimate themselves (Pierre, p. 24). Ignoring information that does not confirm one's beliefs is a difficult bias to control.

Information Disorders

Frissen et al. call for adopting the phrase "information disorders" because all the terms associated with disinformation have become difficult to parse (Frissen et al., p. 275). Information disorder is a phrase that disinformation scholars are adopting to address the problems with using terms like disinformation, fake news, and misinformation to describe the same types of phenomenon. Tumber & Waisbord also refer to disinformation as an “information disorder” (p. 6). Disinformation is designed to lead to information disorders that undermine and replace facts with alternative truths. Many scholars who study disinformation want to use different terms or phrases because of the confusion and misapplication of the terms.


Rebolledo (2025) asserts that one problem with defining all these terms connected to disinformation is their "conceptual ambiguity," which "refers to equivocal associations of terms with the same meaning" (p. 39). She argues that the abuse of terms associated with disinformation leads to more misunderstanding. Information disorders capture the environments and infrastructures of disinformation with more detail and accuracy (Frissen et al., p. 269). It also highlights the effects of disinformation instead of its intent, and it moves beyond the motives of the bad actor and “considers the outcome as the leading defining principle” (p. 269). Recognizing that these terms are not stable is important because disinformation is always changing its structures and forms.

All these terms provide a way to understand disinformation. Although none of these terms are stable, they allow for deeper and nuanced research on disinformation networks. Scholars studying disinformation benefit from having the broadest definitions for misinformation and disinformation and the ability to expand those definitions when disinformation becomes increasingly more difficult to notice and identify. Many scholars who study disinformation want to use alternative terms.
