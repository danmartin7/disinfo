The SPN Narrative

">
Hannah Arendt from On Lying and Politics

SPN websites contain thousands of sources and documents that repeat the same narratives to support a specific set of core issues. The Center for Media and Democracy discloses that the SPN upholds and supports an “extreme right-wing agenda,” and that every member of the SPN promotes a narrative that seeks “to privatize education, block healthcare reform, restrict workers' rights, roll back environmental protections, and create a tax system that benefits mostly those at the very top level of income” (p. 1). Bennett and Livingston (2021) reiterate that the Koch brothers finance organizations that create narratives for undermining voting rights, labor unions, and workers rights and sowing distrust in legacy media, climate science, and science research. These narratives frame how the SPN designs a disinformation disorder that seeks to replace facts with alternative truths (Bennett and Livingston, 2021, p. 27-28). All the SPN websites repeatedly support privatizing education, dismantling unions, lowering minimum wage, building suburban megacities, and oppossing global warmining with disinformation. Disinformation works best when it can provide “endless resentful interpretations of current events” (Tumber & Waisbord, p. 7). Levinger (2018) maintains that "master narratives' can contribute to making falsehoods more credible than facts" (p. 125) when they are repeated across media platforms because they ground misleading information and give it stability. Repetition can foster a sense of stability from consistency. Kalathil (2020) explains that "narrative laundering" is the "legitimization of created narratives through repetition citations across media," and "boosterism" is the "repetitive content reinforcing the perception that a certain narrative represents a popular point of view" (p. 37). The SPN produces a form of narrative laundering for disinformation from repeating and boosting the same narratives across its websites, reinforcing and legitimizing misleading content.

Dave et al. (2020) conducted a case study on Greta Thunberg and tracked her narratives for climate change across numerous digital spaces and found that "Narratives gain traction when they get picked up by more mainstream figures and sources" and move across alternative and legacy media platforms (p. 6). Getting media outlets to reference or link to a source that comes from a disinformation network is boosterism. The SPN uses spin, propoganda, fake news, and misinformation to extend these narratives so that over time they compete with, and possibly, displace common and accepted truths. SPN affiliates vigirously promote the same policies and values on their websites. The redundancy of this agenda forms a foundational layer in the disinformation network. The redundancy and repetition of the SPN narrative across its affiliate websites sends a unified message of national support for these policies. Seeing, hearing, and reading these messages over and over across platforms makes them real and pressing to audiences. 64 different organizations promoting the same messages across a network of platforms gives audiences the assumption that there is broad support for these narratives. The repetition gives the SPN an appearance of authority and relevancy and the appearance of wide support for a set of selected issues and narratives. March (2010) reminds us that “Credibility thrives on familiarity” and not on truth or facts (p. 56). For example, the Arkansas Policy Foundation (APF) website “analyzes the impact of public policy on Arkansas and makes recommendations” on tax policies and education reform. The Yankee Institute (YI), an SPN affiliate in Connecticut, “develops and advances free-market, limited-government solutions in Connecticut. As one of America's oldest state-based think tanks, YI is a leading advocate for smart, limited government; fairness for taxpayers; and an open road to opportunity.” The Alaska Policy Forum's mantra states, “Our vision is an Alaska that continuously grows prosperity by maximizing individual opportunities and freedom.” These types of statements are designed to relate to all Americans and do not suggest the use or presence of disinformation. These types of statements and narratives are a primer and form of calibrating an audience to accept certain claims as honest and urgent. The repetition of these types of statements across platforms leads to what Benkler et al. call a "Propaganda Feedback Loop" that makes it impossible for politicians and media outlets to keep a consistently honest approach to news and content when they are are punished for not promoting a shared narrative that is misleading (p. 33). A feedback loop sanitizes disinformation and forces media outlets outside the SPN to, at the very least, acknowledge the existence of these lies.

SPN websites contain numerous articles addressing a range of issues from anti-grocery bag taxes to anti-union and anti-teacher pension laws. For example, the articles on the California Policy Center's (CPC) website cover topics on anti-union and anti-homelessness, public school limitations, voting rights, and the value of protecting business interests and property rights. These issues are not misleading on the surface, but the evidence and arguments used to support them is problematic. Tumber and Waisbord (2021) insist that disinformation is built on “supplying endless resentful interpretations of current events” (p. 7) that rely on falsehoods and misleading content for support. In Contemporary Conspiracy Culture Harambam notes six categories for conspiracies that directly align with the SPN's narratives: finance, media, corporatism, science, government, and the supernatural (p. 58 ). All the SPN narratives are built on these six categories. On the CPC website's education page, audiences can find articles about how “Public Education Is Changing” and “School Choice Ascending” that contain disinformation. Audiences are consistently exposed to misleading information about “The Coming Upheaval in Education,” “Rethinking Diversity Bureaucrats," and "Rethinking College Education.” Dave et al. explain that "The volume of a specific mis/disinformative narrative helps determine its relevance within the wider public discourse around a given topic" (p. 13). The more often people see the same misleading narratives across the media, the more those narratives become part of the media fabric and compete with legitimate arguments. Spreading the same misleading narrative across multiple platforms over a long period of time is a more accurate characteristic of how disinformation functions. Misinforming people over a long period of time to gain power over them is the primary exigence of a disinformation campaign. Competing narratives for truth create opportunities for circulating misleading information.

The SPN also posts disclaimers on its websites to avoid legal responsibility for producing misleading statements. SPN members like the Pacific Research Institute (PRI) provide disclaimers that emphasize that it does not support the views of its own publications. For example, the PRI website states, “Nothing contained in this blog is to be construed as necessarily reflecting the views of Pacific Research Institute or as an attempt to thwart or aid the passage of any legislation.” Disclaimers like this can be found on all of the SPN websites.
